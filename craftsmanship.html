From Thread to Treasure

Every Andham saree undergoes a meticulous journey spanning weeks to months. Understanding this process helps you appreciate the true value of handloom.

01

Yarn Selection

The journey begins with selecting the finest mulberry silk threads. We source our silk from verified suppliers in Karnataka and Tamil Nadu, ensuring each strand meets our standards for sheen, strength, and softness.

Each batch is tested for purity‚Äîgenuine silk burns to ash and smells like burnt hair, while synthetic fibers melt. This traditional test is still our first quality check.

  • Mulberry silk grade A and above
  • Denier count: 20/22D for warp, 2-ply for weft
  • Natural protein fiber with 18 amino acids
02

Natural Dyeing

We use both traditional natural dyes and certified azo-free chemical dyes. Natural dyes come from plants‚Äîindigo for blue, madder root for red, turmeric for yellow, and pomegranate rinds for greens.

The yarn is boiled in dye baths for hours, then cooled, washed, and dried naturally in shade. This process is repeated for rich, deep colors that don't fade.

  • 100% color fastness testing
  • Natural dyes for specialty collections
  • Shade matching done by master dyers
03

Warping & Winding

Thousands of threads are measured and wound onto beams. For a standard Kanjeevaram, this means 2,000-3,000 warp threads, each 6.5 meters long, arranged with mathematical precision.

The warp threads determine the length of the saree, while weft threads create the width and pattern. This setup alone takes 2-3 days for complex designs.

  • Hand-operated warping mills
  • Tension controlled manually
  • Sectional warping for complex patterns
04

The Weaving

The actual weaving happens on pit looms or frame looms, many over 50 years old. The weaver pedals with their feet, throws the shuttle by hand, and beats each weft thread into place.

A skilled weaver produces 4-6 inches per day for complex Kanjeevarams with zari work. Simple cotton sarees may progress 1-2 feet daily. The sound of the loom‚Äîthak-thak-thak‚Äîis the heartbeat of our craft.

  • Interlocking weft technique
  • Manual zari interlacing
  • 3-ply silk for durability
05

Finishing & Quality Check

The woven fabric is washed in plain water to remove starch, then dried in shade. It's ironed using traditional coal-heated irons or modern steam presses for delicate silks.

Every saree undergoes three inspections: for weaving defects, color consistency, and overall finish. Only then is it folded, packed with neem leaves for protection, and prepared for you.

  • Manual defect checking
  • Steam pressing for sheen
  • Neem leaf packaging (natural moth repellent)

Weaving Techniques

Korvai Technique

A traditional method where the body and border are woven separately and then interlocked with extra weft threads. This creates the signature "temple" or "thazhampoo" border that doesn't separate even after decades of wear.

Time: 15-20 days

Petni

The joining technique where three shuttles are used simultaneously‚Äîtwo for the border and one for the body. This creates the distinct double-shaded borders in Kanjeevaram sarees.

Time: 20-25 days

Jacquard Weaving

Using perforated cards that control individual warp threads, creating complex motifs like peacocks, parrots, and floral patterns. Each card represents one row of the design.

Time: 25-30 days

Zari Work

Pure silver threads coated in gold are interwoven to create shimmering borders and motifs. The percentage of zari varies‚Äîtested zari contains 57% silver and 0.6% gold.

Time: 30-45 days

Kadwa

A painstaking technique where each motif is woven individually with small shuttles, allowing for intricate designs without floating threads on the reverse. Used in Banarasi and high-end Kanjeevarams.

Time: 45-60 days

Hand Spinning

For our Khadi and cotton collections, yarn is still spun by hand on charkhas (spinning wheels), creating a texture that machine-spun yarn cannot replicate.

Time: 7-10 days
